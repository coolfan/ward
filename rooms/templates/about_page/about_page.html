Room Draw Guide

Getting Started
Typically in early March, room draw applications go live at my undergraduate housing, and are usually up for a few weeks. What this means is you need to decide what draws to participate in, organize draw groups for those draws, and apply before that the application goes down. For rising sophmores, that first step is easy - you can only apply to the draw for your residential college. Rising juniors and seniors, on the other hand, can apply to any of the six residential college draws, the independent draw, the upperclass draw, or the Spelman draw. Stayed tuned for more details about the differences between the draws.
For each draw you apply to, you need to create a draw group. Your draw group could be the same across every draw you apply to, or different every time, and must be between one to eight people (except the Wilson draw goes up to ten people, as Wilson has a ten room suite). Everyone in a draw group has the same draw time for that draw. Most importantly, you will only be able to select a room with people in your draw group for that room's draw - so make sure you pick people you like! Note that for those in multiple draws, a draw group isn't an all or nothing type of thing - a subset of your draw group can pick a room for that draw, while the rest pass and wait for a different draw.
Before the Draw

You setup your draw groups, and sent off the applications. It's early April, and draw times are finally released - but how are they chosen? It's pretty simple - every draw group has a point value, which is the average of the points of each individual in the group. Groups with higher point values always draw before those with lower point values, and the order is chosen randomly when there is a tie.

So how do you compute and individual's point value? A main contributor is class year - rising sophomores have a base value of 1 point, rising juniors a base value of 2 points, and rising seniors a base value of 3 points.

For residential college draws, for each year you lived in that residential college or its sister college, you get an additional 0.1 points, to encourage people to stay in their home res colleges.

For the independent and spelman draws, you get an additional 2 points if you sign the independent pledge, which basically is a commitment not to get a University meal plan or get food from an eating club. More details on that coming soon.

You just got all your draw times, and its time to prepare to the big moment. You know your draw time is only a couple of minutes long, depending on the with the size of your draw group. If you don't pick in that time interval, the next group will be able to select rooms while you still are deciding, possibly nabbing the one you were going for out from your very nose. Being a responsible and well prepared Princeton student, you decide to do a bit of planning before that time to figure out what rooms you really want.

This is where WARD comes in! Use the search page to browse through the rooms in your draw, filter and sort based on qualities you care about, read room reviews by those who lived in that room for a whole year, see photos of the rooms, and seamlessly share your findings with the rest of your draw group by favoriting rooms.

After picking out some likely candidates, consider doing a bit of ground work and visiting the room yourself, to get a feel for what it's like living there for a year. People are always happy to give a tour!

I Choose You, *Insert Draw Type Here*!

The Residential College Draws usually occur after the Independent and Spelman draws and before the Upperclass draw.

As I mentioned when discussing the point system, those who lived in a residential college for longer have an advantage when drawing into that college. However, for But there are other considerations when drawing into a residential college.

The first thing to note is that residential colleges have quotas for the maximum amount of juniors and the maximum amount of seniors that can draw into that college, which can vary from college to college and from year to year. These are in place to make sure sophmores drawing into that college still can get good rooms.

The big difference between a residential college draw and other draws is that, if you end up selecting a room in a residential college, you have to get a meal plan, meaning you can't go independent. Some eating clubs have a limited supply of shared meal plans, which do count. Also note that as a rising junior or senior, one can select the Block 90 plan and still do a significant share of one's eating outside of the dining halls.

The Spelman and Independent draws are usually the first and second draws to occur, respectivley. That means you can usually just sign up and wait to see if there are any good rooms available, and if there aren't any, just continue to one of the other draws.

When you make your draw group for the Independent or Spelman draw, you will be asked if you want to sign the independent pledge, which reads:

“My signature on the Housing/Dining Agreement acknowledges my full understanding of the University’s definition of an Independent. I certify by my signature that I am aware if I fail to abide by the Pledge, I will be referred to the Office of the Dean of Undergraduate students for disciplinary action. If I am found in violation of the Independent Pledge, I understand the penalty is normally probation. I am aware that the probation is a matter of permanent record.”

According to the university website an independent is "A student who provides his/her own meals. Independents choose not to contract with Dining Services or join an off-campus eating club or eating co-op, and cannot participate in Residential College or Upperclass Room Draws." This definition is wrong. An independent can join a co-op, and can join an eating club as a social member only. I bring this up to demonstrate the risks of taking anything the university says about room draw at face value. (Indeed, another part of the university website does not mention the prohibition against joining a co-op, but it still wrong about social membership)

It is important to note that you are only obligated to follow the pledge if you end up selecting an independent room. Signing the independent pledge does more than boost your draw time. Any Independent/Spelman room needs at least half of the members to have signed the independent pledge, and to even be able to sign up for the draw, at least half of you draw group needs to sign the pledge.

The Spelam draw is usually immensly popular, and so almost all of the Spelman rooms are typically drawn by rising senior draw groups. This is because all rooms in the Spelman draw contain four bedrooms, a kitchen, and a sizeable living room. As all rooms are quads, only four person and eight person draw groups are allowed to enter the Spelman draw.

The Upperclass Draw is usually the final draw, occuring after the independent draws and the residential college draws. If you still haven't chosen a room by the time the draw rolls around, you will have to do so, unless you want to join the waitlist.

Any junior or senior can apply to the upperclass draw, with no extra restrictions or bonuses. If you happen to select an Upperclass room, and were in the bottom quarter of your class year's draw times, you may apply for room improvement, which is similar to the waitlist - you express your preferences, and if they have a room that meets those preferences that wasn't drawn at all, housing might give it to you.

Picking Your Room

15 minutes before your draw time, housing will allow you to search their website to see which rooms have already been drawn. That isn't a lot of time, especially as the website is notoriously slow and unitituitive, with an unfortunate tendancy to crash at unoportune moments, so it's best to have a list of backup rooms your favorite is already drawn. WARD's likelihood feature can help you here, by giving you an idea of how popular rooms have been in previous years.

When picking a room, everyone who is going to live there has to individually log into the housing website and pick it. This can be hard if your draw time is when you are taking a midterm or a sports practice, or any other event you cannot miss. Luckily, housing allows you to designate one other person as your proxy, who you empower to select your room for you.

When the time finally arrives, you log into my undergraduate housing.

A link should pop up on the home page, inviting you to select your room. After you pick a room, you are given a chance to select a meal plan and agree to the rule's of subfree housing, if applicable. Once the room has been completley filled, you are done! Congratulations, you survived room draw.
